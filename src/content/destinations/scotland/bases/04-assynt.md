You come off the CalMac boat at Ullapool with the car still smelling faintly of the crossing, and the first thing that registers isn't a landmark — it's the light. Late afternoon in early June out here runs long and low, and the road north from the ferry slip already has Assynt's lone mountains on the horizon, laid out like something that shouldn't be geologically possible: sugar-loaf summits rising straight out of peat bog with nothing around them. You don't stop for any of it yet. You drive to Lochinver, drop the bags, and walk down to [[poi:assynt-peets]] on the harbour, because five nights here run on one working theory — eat well first, sightsee around it.

The wheelhouse version of that theory is [[poi:assynt-seafood-shack]], forty minutes south in Ullapool: a servery, not a restaurant, food handed across a counter into your hands while boats you can still see are tied up thirty feet away. You eat standing at the harbour wall with the scallops still hot, and it's a better lunch than most tasting menus manage in three courses. The rest of the days fall into a rhythm around it — a morning at [[poi:assynt-achmelvich]] before the convoys arrive, water that photographs turquoise and isn't lying about it; the broch at [[poi:assynt-clachtoll]]; the falls at [[poi:assynt-clashnessie]] that almost nobody walks to; and an afternoon on the short leg toward [[poi:assynt-falls-of-kirkaig]], where Suilven appears at the top of the moor the way mountains aren't supposed to — all at once, no foothills to warn you.

The [[poi:assynt-drumbeg-loop]] is where the driving itself becomes the point: twenty-two miles of single-track, blind summits, hairpins with the sea on one side and bare rock on the other — rated harder than the Applecross pass, which is exactly why the motorhome convoys stay away. You take it slow, you reverse into passing places without complaint, and near the northern end [[poi:assynt-wailing-widow-falls]] gives you a reason to get out and stretch. On the Coigach day you run the same idea in reverse — up [[poi:assynt-stac-pollaidh]] for a two-hour push to a saddle overlooking half of Assynt and every one of the Summer Isles, then down to the pier for [[poi:assynt-summer-isles-boat]], seals hauled out on the skerries, and dinner after at [[poi:assynt-am-fuaran]] with langoustines that were in a creel that morning.

None of it needs a museum. The history here — [[poi:assynt-bone-caves-ardvreck]], Ice Age bones and a ruined tower on a loch — sits in the background exactly where it should, a backdrop for the drive rather than a destination in its own right. What actually holds five nights together is water you keep wanting to swim in and can't quite justify at Highland temperatures, a pie that won a national award forty years into the making, and light that doesn't fully leave the sky until close to eleven — so the last beach of the day is always still going when everyone else has gone in for dinner. On the final morning you point the car at Inverness, stop at Glen Ord for one last dram if the flight allows, and let the trip end the way it ran: unhurried.
