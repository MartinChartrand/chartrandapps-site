You'll smell the smokehouse before you see it. Leith doesn't announce itself the way the Old Town does — no bagpipes, no tartan shopfronts, no menu translated into six languages and taped to a window. It announces itself with oak smoke drifting off a grill on The Shore, with the tide smell of a working harbour, with a queue of actual Leithers waiting for a table at [[poi:edinburgh-dulse-leith]] because the seafood boil is on. This was a shipping port long before it was a place anyone came for atmosphere, and it still runs on that logic: the fish is good because the fish arrives here first.

Base yourself in Leith or Stockbridge and skip the Royal Mile entirely for the first few days — you're not missing anything you can't see in an afternoon later. What you get instead is [[poi:edinburgh-fishers-leith]] at the foot of an old windmill, a platter of hot-smoked salmon and rollmop herring that requires no ceremony, and a five-minute walk home along the water. In late May the festival crowds mercifully don't exist yet — the Fringe doesn't start until August, and Edinburgh in May is a different, quieter city that knows something the August crowds don't.

The water here isn't scenery, it's the plan. [[poi:edinburgh-portobello-beach]] is Edinburgh's actual beach — sand, a promenade, a wild-swimming crowd that treats the North Sea as a reasonable place to be in May — and a few steps from it, [[poi:edinburgh-soul-water-sauna]] runs a wood-fired sauna where the whole point is walking straight from the heat into the cold water and back until you stop counting. Newhaven does the same trick smaller: a working harbour with its own lighthouse, and [[poi:edinburgh-the-fishmarket-newhaven]] serving fish and chips out of a hatch to eat on the harbour wall. None of this requires a car, a booking three months out, or a costume. It just requires showing up.

Slow travel means the morning walk matters as much as the dinner reservation. [[poi:edinburgh-dean-village]] at 7am — empty, cobbled, the Water of Leith running past converted grain mills — leads you upstream toward Stockbridge past a small Greek temple over a mineral spring that most people walk past without noticing. Sunday morning belongs to [[poi:edinburgh-stockbridge-market]], forty-odd stalls of Scottish cheese and local veg on Saunders Street. And one morning, set the early alarm for [[poi:edinburgh-arthurs-seat-sunrise]] — the one classic Edinburgh sight worth doing the classic way, because at dawn the extinct volcano in the middle of the city belongs to you and nobody else.

History shows up sideways, which is exactly how it should. [[poi:edinburgh-port-of-leith-distillery]] is nine storeys of working distillery on the docks, not a heritage centre — you fill your own miniature bottle and taste a proper dram from the 8th-floor bar with the working port spread out below, which is a better whisky story than anything on the Royal Mile. Circus Lane and the overgrown Victorian graves at Warriston are the kind of thing you find by accident on a slow afternoon, not the kind of thing you queue for. That's the whole idea of this base: the food and the water do the talking, and history gets to be the thing you notice out of the corner of your eye while you're doing something else.
