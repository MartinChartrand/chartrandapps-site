You don't so much arrive on Harris as get delivered to it — the Tarbert pier appearing out of ninety minutes of open Minch, the MV Hebrides swinging in past the loch, and then the car's wheels are on Harris tarmac and there's nowhere else to be for five days. The first hour on the island rarely goes further than the nearest window: everyone warns you about [[poi:harris-luskentyre-seilebost]] and everyone is still not ready for it. The sand doesn't end so much as dissolve into water that changes colour while you're standing in it, and Taransay sits offshore like a rumour. You turn fifty here, or close enough to it, and there's a specific kind of relief in a landscape that asks nothing of you except to look.

The food does not perform for you, which is the whole point. [[poi:harris-croft36]] is a shed with an honesty box and a langoustine buttery that sells out by early afternoon — nobody's watching you eat it, there's no photo opportunity built in, you just get in line behind two crofters and wait your turn. [[poi:harris-north-harbour-bistro]] on Scalpay serves whatever came off the creel boats that morning, which means the menu is really just today's weather translated into shellfish. And when you want the version of this island that took forty years to build a reputation, [[poi:harris-scarista-house]] sets a candlelit no-choice menu of island fish and machair lamb, looking out at three miles of beach that nobody's advertising. That's the range on Harris — an honesty box and a set menu, and both are the same honesty, really.

Driving here is its own kind of company. The [[poi:harris-golden-road]] on the east coast is barely a road at all, switching back through rock that geologists genuinely compare to the moon's surface, past inlets too small to have proper harbours. The [[poi:harris-hushinish]] run is worse and better — twelve miles of single-track, sheep contesting every blind corner, ending at a beach that looks like a reward for surviving the drive. You stop at [[poi:harris-st-clements-rodel]] at the very bottom of the island, where a five-hundred-year-old tomb sits inside a small stone church and the graveyard around it holds more silence than anywhere else you've been in years.

None of it is trying to convince you of anything. That's what a slow week here amounts to — a lighthouse walk on [[poi:harris-scalpay-eilean-glas]] that takes half a day and rewards you with a coffee shop and nothing else nearby, a gin at [[poi:harris-distillery]] distilled from kelp somebody dove for themselves, a whisky that only exists because this specific island finally got around to bottling one, and — if the Atlantic allows — a boat to [[poi:harris-boat-trip]] at the edge of the world. You leave from Stornoway on the Saturday, the ferry pointed back toward Ullapool, and the water between here and there feels considerably wider going home than it did coming out.
