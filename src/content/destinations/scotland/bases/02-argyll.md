You leave Edinburgh behind at the Rest and Be Thankful, the old drovers' pass where the road hairpins up out of Glen Croe and the trees just stop — heather and rock and, more often than not, a low cloud sitting on the col like it's been asked to stay. Coming down the other side into Cairndow, you're already at [[poi:argyll-loch-fyne-oyster-bar]], the shed on the water's edge that put Loch Fyne oysters on the map back in 1978 — a dozen shucked to order before you've even reached your base for the next five nights. That's the right way to arrive in Argyll: eating before you've unpacked.

Tighnabruaich sits where the Kyles of Bute narrow into three channels around the top of the island, and the [[poi:argyll-kyles-viewpoint]] above the village will tell you everything you need to know about why people who've never heard of Cowal end up staying a week. This is the "Secret Coast" — not because anyone's hiding it, but because the road in only goes one way, and most of the country never bothers. You'll get the rhythm of it fast: single track, passing places, a wave for every car that pulls in to let you by. The [[poi:argyll-secret-coast-loop]] south past Millhouse and Ardlamont is the drive that proves it — seals, oystercatchers, no traffic, and at the end of it, [[poi:argyll-ostel-bay]], a curve of sand facing Arran that most maps barely bother to name.

Dinner doesn't ask you to go far, but it rewards you when you do. Twenty minutes up the coast road, [[poi:argyll-oystercatcher]] sits with its tables angled at the loch. A short CalMac hop across Loch Fyne — the [[poi:argyll-portavadie-tarbert-ferry]], twenty-five minutes, turn up and go — puts you in Kintyre for langoustines at [[poi:argyll-starfish-tarbert]] on Tarbert's working harbour, or further south at [[poi:argyll-skipness-seafood-cabin]], where you eat dressed crab off a picnic table in the shadow of a 13th-century castle ruin. And if you're willing to drive further still, [[poi:argyll-kilberry-inn]] — the one Bib Gourmand on this coast — is worth the single-track detour on its own.

The history is here if you want it, but it stays in its lane: [[poi:argyll-inveraray]] early, before the coach parties arrive, on the same road as your oyster-bar lunch; [[poi:argyll-crinan-kilmartin]] on a slower day, where a canal full of yachts and eight hundred prehistoric stones share a single afternoon without either one asking to be the main event. And for one night, let yourself be a tourist of comfort instead of place: [[poi:argyll-portavadie]], where Scotland's biggest outdoor infinity pool sits at the loch's edge, a short hop from the [[poi:argyll-bute-distillery]] gin distilled with actual Loch Fyne oyster shells. It's the kind of detail that tells you where you are, even with your eyes closed.
